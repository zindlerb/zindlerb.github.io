Notes on Communicating with Interactive Articles

Very rough notes on the Distill Pub Article Communicating with Interactive Articles.

Really good survey of the state of interactive essays. Lots of interesting essays that I would like to give a read. Unraveling the JPEG looked especially good.

How much are essays improved by interactivity? I do think there are some essays genuinely aided by interactivity but many examples felt like they would be better with high quality data graphics replacing the interactive elements.

I was reminded of Bret Victor's skepticism of interaction in information software from Magic Ink

Chris Crawford defines interaction as a three-phase reciprocal process, isomorphic to a conversation: an interactant listens to her partner, thinks about what was said, and speaks a response. Her partner then does the same. For manipulation software, interaction is perfectly suitable: the user views a visual representation of the model, considers what to manipulate next, and performs a manipulation. The software, in turn, inputs the user’s manipulation request, updates the model, and displays the updated representation. With good feedback and an effective means of “speaking” to the software, this process can cycle smoothly and rapidly. It mimics the experience of working with a physical tool. Information software, by contrast, mimics the experience of reading, not working. It is used for achieving an understanding—constructing a model within the mind. Thus, the user must listen to the software and think about what it says… but any manipulation happens mentally. The only reason to complete the full interaction cycle and speak is to explicitly provide some context that the software can’t otherwise infer—that is, to indicate a relevant subset of information. For information software, all interaction is essentially navigation around a data space.

One of the cited essays Coeffects: Context-aware programming languages allows the user to choose how much content to show by default:

This interactivity is similar to what might be done via graphic design via highlighting an low-lighting information. This made me think about the hierarchy of tools a designer has for organizing information. Some methods that came to mind (sorted from least to most user effort):

Non-interactive

  • Type heirarchy
  • Highlighting/Lowlighting text

Interactive

  • Hovering to reveal content
  • Clicking to reveal + hide content
  • Navigating to another page via link

Thinking about the revealing and hiding content, lead me to think about the boundaries of "Browsers" and "Editors" and "Documents".

"Documents" being knowledge that someone has posted in some form like a webpage.

"Browsers" being a program for viewing documents

"Editors" being a program for manipulating documents

For an interactive article the "document" is the webpage and the "browser" is the web browser. The interactive articles are taking on a lot of the responsibility of the interaction.

What if this responsibility was shifted onto the "browser". For example what if my browser for webpages looked more like LiquidText? And I could scrunch up sections of text. This accomplishes a similar purpose to allowing the reader to show and hide text via buttons in the article. But if the browser gives me this capability I can do it anywhere.

But shifting this behaviour to the browser may mean constraining what is possible in the "document".

How would having a "browser" like Andy Matuschak's notes effect the degree of interaction needed in an essay? Do you need to do as much showing and hiding if cost of navigating a link is not as high?

Programs like Notion and Roam seem to be a fusion of "browser", "document", and "editor". But, that comes at the expense of constraining the layouts that are possible.

How can we build tools that democratize the creation of interactive articles?

It could be hugely beneficial for domain experts to be able to author interactive articles without programming knowledge. But, the space of interactivity is so wide. It's hard to create a tool for creating interactive "explorables" that is both flexible and powerful.

We still don't know what makes a good interactive essay so the range of expressible interaction should ideally be as wide as possible.

One approach may be to first nail how do we give non-programmers and non-designers the ability to control the layout and organization of their content.

Currently someone can't add Tufte style references to their blogpost without being reasonably familiar with html/css.
